# Copyright Guidelines 

## Hong Kong Institute of Information Technology (HKIIT) 

### Effective Date: `2026-03-01` 

 

#### 1. Introduction 

The Hong Kong Institute of Information Technology ("HKIIT" or "the Institute") is committed to fostering an environment of academic integrity and respect for intellectual property. These Copyright Guidelines are designed to provide clarity and guidance to all members of the HKIIT community – including students, faculty, staff, and researchers – regarding their rights and responsibilities concerning copyrighted materials. 

Understanding and adhering to copyright law is crucial to avoid legal liabilities and uphold ethical academic and professional standards. These guidelines are intended to promote awareness and facilitate compliance with the Copyright Ordinance (Cap. 528) of Hong Kong. 

 

#### 2. What is Copyright? 

Copyright is a legal right that protects original literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works. It grants the creator (or copyright holder) exclusive rights to control the use and distribution of their work. 

Types of Works Protected by Copyright (under Hong Kong law): 

Literary Works: Books, articles, software code, lectures, theses, website content, databases. 

Dramatic Works: Plays, scripts, dance, mime. 

Musical Works: Compositions, scores. 

Artistic Works: Paintings, drawings, photographs, sculptures, architectural designs, maps, diagrams, graphics, films, sound recordings, broadcasts. 

Key Characteristics of Copyright: 

Automatic Protection: Copyright protection is automatic upon creation of an original work; no registration is required in Hong Kong. 

Originality: The work must be original, meaning it is not copied from another source and involved some independent effort or skill. 

Fixed Form: The work must be expressed in a tangible form (e.g., written down, recorded, drawn). 

Duration: Generally, copyright in Hong Kong lasts for the life of the author plus 50 years after their death. For films, sound recordings, and broadcasts, it's 50 years from the end of the year in which they were made or first published/broadcast. 

What Copyright Does NOT Protect: 

Ideas, concepts, facts, methods, systems, or processes (only the expression of these is protected). 

Titles, names, short phrases, slogans (these may be protected by trademark law, but not copyright). 

Works that are not original or are in the public domain. 

 

#### 3. Copyright Ownership at HKIIT 

Understanding who owns copyright is fundamental: 

General Rule: The Creator Owns Copyright. Unless specific agreements or employment terms state otherwise, the person who creates an original work is generally the first owner of the copyright. 

Works Created by HKIIT Employees (Faculty and Staff): 

Works created by faculty or staff in the course of their employment (e.g., course syllabi, lecture notes, administrative documents, research conducted as part of their job duties) are generally owned by HKIIT as the employer, unless an employment contract or specific institutional policy dictates otherwise. 

Research publications and scholarly articles are often subject to specific institutional policies or publisher agreements, which may allow faculty to retain certain rights while granting the institution or publisher a license. 

Works Created by Students: 

Students generally own the copyright to their academic work (e.g., assignments, essays, theses, dissertations, projects) created as part of their studies at HKIIT. 

Exceptions: 

If a student's work is created under a specific agreement that transfers copyright to HKIIT or a third party (e.g., sponsored research projects). 

If the student's work is a "work for hire" under a separate contract with HKIIT. 

If the student's work significantly incorporates copyrighted material owned by HKIIT (e.g., using extensive proprietary software developed by the Institute). 

HKIIT Institutional Content: 

All official HKIIT materials, including its website content, logos, branding, marketing materials, official publications, and institutional software, are owned by HKIIT. 

 

#### 4. Using Copyrighted Materials Owned by Others (Inbound) 

When you wish to use someone else's copyrighted material, the general rule is to seek permission from the copyright holder. However, Hong Kong copyright law provides certain exceptions, notably "Fair Dealing." 

4.1. General Principles for Using Others' Work 

Assume it's Copyrighted: If you didn't create it, assume it's copyrighted unless proven otherwise (e.g., clearly marked as public domain or under an open license). 

Identify the Copyright Holder: Look for copyright notices (©), author names, or publisher information. 

Seek Permission: If your intended use falls outside the "Fair Dealing" exceptions, you must obtain written permission from the copyright holder. This may involve licensing fees. 

Always Attribute: Even when using materials under "Fair Dealing" or open licenses, proper attribution is essential. 

4.2. Fair Dealing (Sections 38-41 of the Hong Kong Copyright Ordinance) 

"Fair Dealing" is an important exception that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission for specific purposes. It is not a blanket right to use any material for educational purposes. Each use must be assessed on a case-by-case basis. 

Permitted Purposes for Fair Dealing in Hong Kong (relevant to HKIIT): 

Research and Private Study: Individuals may make a single copy of a reasonable portion of a work for their personal research or private study. 

Criticism or Review: Using a limited portion of a work for the purpose of criticism or review, provided there is sufficient acknowledgment. 

News Reporting: Using a limited portion of a work for the purpose of reporting current events, provided there is sufficient acknowledgment. 

Education: 

Copying by a teacher or student for the purposes of instruction or examination. 

Copying by educational establishments for the purposes of instruction (e.g., making multiple copies for a class). 

Performing, playing, or showing a work in the course of the activities of an educational establishment. 

Factors to Consider for "Fair Dealing": 

When determining if a use constitutes "Fair Dealing," courts typically consider the following factors: 

The purpose and character of the dealing: Is it for non-profit educational use, or for commercial gain? 

The nature of the work: Is it factual or creative? Unpublished or published? 

The amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole: Using a small, insignificant portion is more likely to be fair than using a large or "heart" of the work. 

The effect of the dealing upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work: Does the use substitute for the original, thereby harming the copyright holder's ability to sell or license their work? 

Important Note: Even for educational purposes, extensive copying, making materials permanently available online without restrictions, or using entire works without a strong justification is unlikely to be considered Fair Dealing. 

4.3. Specific Contexts for Using Copyrighted Materials at HKIIT 

Classroom Use (Physical): 

Teachers may make multiple copies of limited extracts from literary, dramatic, or musical works for classroom instruction, provided the copying is not for commercial purposes and sufficient acknowledgment is given. 

Displaying copyrighted images, playing music, or showing films in a physical classroom for instructional purposes is generally permitted. 

Online Learning Platforms (LMS - e.g., Moodle): 

Materials posted on secure, password-protected learning management systems for enrolled students are more likely to fall under educational Fair Dealing than materials posted publicly. 

Best Practices: 

Link to external resources whenever possible, rather than copying and uploading. 

Limit the amount copied to what is strictly necessary for instruction. 

Remove materials once the course or relevant module is completed. 

Always provide proper attribution. 

Avoid posting entire textbooks or significant portions of published works. 

Library Resources: 

HKIIT Library subscribes to many databases, journals, and e-books. These resources are governed by specific license agreements. Users must adhere to these agreements, which typically permit use for personal study, research, and non-commercial educational purposes, but prohibit systematic downloading, redistribution, or commercial use. 

Public Domain: 

Works whose copyright has expired or were never protected by copyright are in the public domain. These can be used freely without permission or attribution (though attribution is still good academic practice). Check the copyright duration carefully. 

Open Licenses (e.g., Creative Commons - CC): 

Some creators choose to license their work under open licenses (e.g., Creative Commons). These licenses specify how the work can be used, often requiring attribution and sometimes restricting commercial use or derivative works. Always check the specific terms of the CC license. 

 

#### 5. Protecting Your Own Copyright (Outbound) 

5.1. For Students: 

You generally own the copyright to your original academic works. 

Protection: Your work is automatically protected. You may choose to include a copyright notice (e.g., "© [Your Name] [Year]") but it is not legally required for protection in Hong Kong. 

Sharing Your Work: If you wish to share your work (e.g., online portfolio, open access thesis), consider using a Creative Commons license to clearly communicate how others may use your work. 

5.2. For Faculty and Staff: 

Course Materials: Materials created in the course of your employment are generally owned by HKIIT. However, the Institute supports the dissemination of knowledge. Discuss with your department head or HKIIT administration regarding specific policies on sharing or publishing your teaching materials outside the Institute. 

Research & Publications: 

Be aware of copyright agreements with publishers when submitting articles or books. Many publishers require you to transfer copyright, but you may be able to negotiate to retain certain rights (e.g., to post a version of your article in an institutional repository). 

Consider using open access journals or repositories that allow you to retain more rights to your work. 

Always include a copyright notice (e.g., "© [Your Name] [Year]") on your original works. 

Using HKIIT's Name and Logo: Use of the HKIIT name, logo, or other institutional branding must adhere to the Institute's branding guidelines and policies. 

 

#### 6. Plagiarism vs. Copyright Infringement 

While related, plagiarism and copyright infringement are distinct concepts: 

Plagiarism: An academic offense involving presenting someone else's ideas or words as your own without proper attribution, regardless of whether the material is copyrighted. It is a breach of academic integrity. 

Copyright Infringement: A legal offense involving the unauthorized use, reproduction, distribution, display, or performance of a copyrighted work. It can occur even if you properly attribute the source, but did not obtain permission for a use that falls outside "Fair Dealing" or other legal exceptions. 

They can overlap: Copying someone else's copyrighted work without permission and without attribution constitutes both copyright infringement and plagiarism. 

 

#### 7. Consequences of Copyright Infringement 

Legal Consequences: Under Hong Kong law, copyright infringement can lead to significant penalties, including: 

Civil Remedies: Injunctions, damages, account of profits, delivery up of infringing articles. 

Criminal Penalties: For commercial-scale infringement, penalties can include substantial fines and imprisonment. 

Academic Consequences (for students): Plagiarism and copyright infringement are serious academic offenses and may result in disciplinary action, including failing grades, suspension, or expulsion. 

Professional Consequences (for faculty/staff): May lead to disciplinary action, reputational damage, and legal liability. 

Institutional Impact: HKIIT could face legal action, financial penalties, and damage to its reputation. 

 

#### 8. Reporting Copyright Infringement 

If you believe that copyrighted material owned by you or HKIIT has been infringed upon through the Services (e.g., on an HKIIT website or learning platform), please notify our designated Copyright Agent with the following information: 

A physical or electronic signature of a person authorized to act on behalf of the copyright owner. 

Identification of the copyrighted work claimed to have been infringed. 

Identification of the material that is claimed to be infringing or to be the subject of infringing activity and that is to be removed or access to which is to be disabled, and information reasonably sufficient to permit HKIIT to locate the material. 

Information reasonably sufficient to permit HKIIT to contact you, such as an address, telephone number, and, if available, an e-mail address. 

A statement that you have a good faith belief that use of the material in the manner complained of is not authorized by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law. 

A statement that the information in the notification is accurate, and under penalty of perjury, that you are authorized to act on behalf of the owner of an exclusive right that is allegedly infringed.
